Goethe A2 Reading (Lesen): Strategies

June 10, 2026 · Goethe A2 · 8 min read

Key takeaways

  • The A2 Lesen module lasts about 30 minutes and has four parts.
  • Texts are short and everyday: emails, ads, notices, signs, and simple articles.
  • You do not need to understand every word — find the specific information each question asks for.
  • Read the questions first, then scan the text for the answer.
  • Watch for matching traps where a word from the text appears in a wrong option.

What the A2 reading exam tests

The Goethe-Zertifikat A2 Lesen gives you about 30 minutes to work through four parts. The texts mirror what you meet in daily life in a German-speaking country: a message from a friend, a small ad, a public notice, a sign, or a short informational text. The reading is elementary, so the challenge is less about vocabulary and more about working quickly and reading the question carefully.

The exam never asks you to understand a whole text in detail. Each question targets one piece of information. Your job is to locate it. That single idea changes how you should read: you are searching, not studying.

The four parts of A2 Lesen

Part 1: A short text with detail questions

You read a short personal text, often an email or message, and answer true/false or multiple-choice questions about it. The questions follow the order of the text, so once you find the answer to one, the next is usually a little further down.

Strategy: read the questions first, underline the key word in each (a time, a place, a name, a reason), then read the text and stop when you hit that information.

Part 2: Ads and notices

You match people or situations to short advertisements or notices. For example, several people want different things, and you decide which ad fits each one — sometimes one situation has no match.

Strategy: read what each person needs first and turn it into one key idea. Then scan the ads for that idea. Do not read every ad word for word; look for the detail that matches or rules it out.

Part 3: Everyday correspondence or signs

You read short practical texts — signs, instructions, or messages — and decide what they mean or whether a statement is correct. These check whether you understand the function of a text: is it allowing something, forbidding it, or giving information?

Strategy: focus on the action words and any negation. A small word like nicht, kein, or nur often decides the answer.

Part 4: A short informational text

You read a slightly longer text on an everyday topic and answer questions about its content. The language stays at A2 level, but there is a bit more to process, so manage your time so you do not rush this part.

Strategy: skim once for the general topic, then go question by question, scanning back to the relevant sentence for each answer.

Core reading strategies for A2

Read the questions before the text

This is the single most useful habit. When you know what you are looking for, your eyes find it faster and you ignore everything irrelevant. Reading the text first and the questions second wastes time you do not have.

Skim and scan, do not translate

Skimming means passing over a text quickly for the gist. Scanning means hunting for a specific word or fact. Both beat slow word-by-word translation. If you stop at every unknown word, you will run out of time and lose focus on the actual question.

Use the words you do know

You will meet unfamiliar words — that is normal and expected. Often the surrounding words and the topic make the meaning clear enough to answer. Guessing from context is a skill the exam rewards, not a weakness.

Mind the matching trap

A wrong option frequently contains a word that also appears in the text, placed there to tempt you. The correct answer usually paraphrases the text rather than repeating it. Match the meaning, not the exact word.

Time management

With about 30 minutes across four parts, roughly seven to eight minutes per part is a safe pace, leaving a few minutes to transfer and check answers. If one question is taking too long, mark your best guess and move on. There is no penalty for a wrong answer, so never leave a blank — a guess can be right, an empty box never is.

Keep an eye on the clock but do not panic. The earlier parts are usually quicker, which gives you a little extra room for Part 4.

Common mistakes in A2 reading

The biggest mistake is over-reading: trying to understand the whole text instead of answering the question in front of you. The second is ignoring negation — readers see a familiar word and assume the answer without noticing a nicht or kein that flips the meaning. The third is leaving questions blank because of one unknown word, when the answer was findable from context.

Train these out by practising with a timer and always checking, after each answer, exactly which sentence in the text justified it. That habit makes you faster and more accurate at the same time.

How to practise

Read short German texts every day — messages, simple news, café menus, signs. For exam practice, do timed Lesen sets and review every mistake: was it a vocabulary gap, a misread question, or a trap? Knowing the cause tells you what to fix.
