Goethe A2 Vocabulary: Essential Words and Themes

June 10, 2026 · Goethe A2 · 8 min read

Key takeaways

  • A2 vocabulary is built around everyday themes: family, work, shopping, health, travel, and free time.
  • Aim for roughly 1,000–1,300 active words — enough to handle routine situations.
  • Learn words in chunks and full sentences, not as isolated translations.
  • Always learn each noun with its article and plural.
  • The past tense you need at A2 is the Perfekt (ich habe gemacht, ich bin gefahren).

How much vocabulary do you need for A2?

The Goethe-Zertifikat A2 tests elementary German. You do not need a huge vocabulary — you need the right one. Around 1,000 to 1,300 well-known words cover most exam tasks, because the content stays close to daily life. The exam rewards depth over breadth: knowing a smaller set of words you can actually use beats recognising a long list you cannot produce.

The goal at A2 is to talk about yourself, your routine, and your immediate surroundings. That means the words you choose should be practical and high-frequency. Skip rare or abstract terms and focus on what comes up in emails, conversations, and announcements.

The core A2 themes

Goethe A2 topics repeat across Lesen, Hören, Schreiben, and Sprechen. Build your word lists around these themes and you cover most of what the exam asks.

People and family (Personen und Familie)

die Familie, die Eltern, der Bruder, die Schwester, verheiratet, ledig, der Freund, die Kollegin. Be ready to describe people: nett, freundlich, ruhig, jung, alt.

Work and study (Arbeit und Ausbildung)

der Beruf, die Arbeit, das Büro, der Termin, die Besprechung, studieren, der Kurs, die Pause. You will often write a short message to a colleague or read a job-related notice.

Home and shopping (Wohnen und Einkaufen)

die Wohnung, das Zimmer, die Miete, der Supermarkt, das Angebot, der Preis, billig, teuer, kaufen, bezahlen. Numbers, prices, and quantities show up constantly here.

Health and the body (Gesundheit)

der Arzt, die Apotheke, krank, die Erkältung, der Termin, weh tun, sich fühlen. A common task is making or changing a doctor's appointment.

Travel and getting around (Reisen und Verkehr)

der Bahnhof, die Fahrkarte, der Zug, umsteigen, die Reise, der Urlaub, das Hotel, ankommen, abfahren. Directions and timetables are typical listening material.

Free time and daily routine (Freizeit und Alltag)

das Hobby, der Sport, das Kino, sich treffen, der Termin, das Wochenende, fernsehen, spazieren gehen. This is the most common writing and speaking topic at A2.

High-value words that connect ideas

A short list of connectors lifts your writing and speaking immediately. At A2 you are not expected to argue, but you should link sentences clearly.

  • und, aber, oder — and, but, or
  • weil — because (verb goes to the end)
  • deshalb — therefore
  • zuerst, dann, danach, später — first, then, after that, later
  • denn — because (in main-clause order)

These small words make a big difference. A response that uses weil and dann reads far more naturally than a string of short, disconnected sentences.

Learn nouns the right way

Every German noun has a gender, and at A2 examiners notice when articles are wrong. Never learn a noun alone — learn it as a three-part unit: article, word, plural.

  • der Tisch, die Tische
  • die Tasche, die Taschen
  • das Fenster, die Fenster

Writing the article in a colour or always saying it aloud helps it stick. When you guess later, you will guess from memory of the chunk, not from a rule.

Verbs and the Perfekt

A2 introduces the past tense, and the form you need most is the Perfekt. Learn each verb together with its participle and its helper verb (haben or sein).

  • machen → ich habe gemacht
  • essen → ich habe gegessen
  • fahren → ich bin gefahren
  • gehen → ich bin gegangen

Movement verbs and verbs of change (fahren, gehen, kommen, bleiben) take sein. Most others take haben. Memorise the common irregular participles as vocabulary, because they appear in almost every writing and speaking task.

A simple method that works

Vocabulary sticks when you meet it often and use it in context. This routine takes 20 minutes a day and fits the A2 timeline.

  1. Group by theme. Study 10–15 words from one topic at a time, not random lists.
  2. Write one sentence per word. A word you have used is far easier to recall than a word you have only read.
  3. Use spaced repetition. Review with an app or flashcards so old words come back before you forget them.
  4. Say it out loud. Speaking the word fixes pronunciation and helps in the Sprechen module.
  5. Recycle in writing. When you practise a Schreiben task, deliberately reuse this week's words.

Common vocabulary mistakes at A2

Two patterns cost easy points. First, mixing up similar words such as besuchen (to visit a person/place) and besichtigen (to tour a sight). Second, false friends like bekommen (to receive, not to become). When you note a new word, note what it does not mean if it looks like an English word.

Also watch separable verbs: aufstehen, einkaufen, anrufen. In a main clause the prefix jumps to the end — "Ich stehe um sieben Uhr auf." Learning the verb in a full sentence trains this automatically.

Put it together

Strong A2 vocabulary is practical, themed, and used out loud. Cover the core topics, learn nouns with their articles, and build a small set of reliable Perfekt verbs and connectors.
