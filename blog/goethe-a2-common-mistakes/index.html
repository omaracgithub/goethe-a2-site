10 Common Mistakes in the Goethe A2 Exam

June 10, 2026 · Goethe A2 · 9 min read

Key takeaways

  • Most lost points at A2 come from avoidable mistakes, not from a lack of German.
  • In writing, the biggest one is missing a required content point.
  • In speaking, candidates often fail to interact with their partner.
  • Across all modules, blank answers and poor time management cost easy marks.
  • You need 60 of 100 points to pass, so a few saved points decide results.

The Goethe-Zertifikat A2 is an elementary exam, and most candidates have the German they need to pass. What separates a pass from a fail is usually a handful of repeated, avoidable errors. Here are the ten that cost the most points, with a fix for each.

1. Missing a required content point in Schreiben

Each writing task gives you points you must address. Skip one and your task-fulfilment score drops sharply, no matter how good the rest is. Before you start, list the points from the prompt. After you write, check each one off. This single habit protects a large share of your writing marks.

2. Using the wrong register

A2 writing tasks are either informal (a message to a friend, using du) or formal (a note to an institution, using Sie). Mixing them — informal greetings in a formal message, or vice versa — is heavily noticed. Decide the register first and keep your greeting, pronouns, and closing consistent with it.

3. Leaving answers blank

There is no negative marking on the Goethe A2. A blank in Lesen or Hören is a guaranteed zero; a guess might be right. Always fill in every answer, even when you are unsure. In the last minute, make sure no box is empty.

4. Trying to understand every word

In Lesen and Hören you do not need full comprehension — you need the specific detail each question asks for. Candidates who stop at every unknown word run out of time and miss easy points. Read the question first, then search the text or audio for that one piece of information.

5. Not reading the questions before the audio or text

The short time before a listening clip plays, or before you read a passage, is your biggest advantage. Use it to read the questions and underline key words. Going in without knowing what to look for means you process everything and catch nothing in particular.

6. Panicking after missing one listening answer

Hören audio moves on whether you are ready or not. If you miss one answer and freeze on it, you will miss the next as well. Let it go immediately, mark your best guess, and focus on the current question. One missed item is recoverable; a chain reaction is not.

7. Giving one-word answers in Sprechen

The speaking module is paired, and interaction is part of the score. Candidates who answer with single words, never ask their partner anything, or ignore what their partner said lose points even when their German is correct. Ask follow-up questions, react to your partner, and keep the exchange going.

8. Weak past tense (Perfekt)

A2 expects the Perfekt for past events, and errors here are common: wrong helper verb (haben vs sein) or a wrong participle. Learn movement verbs with sein (ich bin gefahren, ich bin gegangen) and the frequent irregular participles (gegessen, getrunken, gesehen) as fixed vocabulary, so they come out right under pressure.

9. Wrong articles and noun genders

Saying or writing the wrong article (der/die/das) is a frequent A2 slip. The fix is in how you learn vocabulary: never learn a noun alone. Always learn it with its article and plural — der Tisch / die Tische — so the gender is part of the word in your memory. See our A2 vocabulary guide for the method.

10. Poor time management

Each written module runs about 30 minutes, and spending too long on one task starves the others. Set a rough time per part and stick to it. If something is taking too long, make your best attempt and move on. Leave a couple of minutes at the end of each module to transfer and check answers.

Putting it together

None of these mistakes require more German to fix — they require better exam habits. Read questions first, fill every answer, address every content point, keep the conversation going, and watch your time. Those habits routinely turn a borderline score into a comfortable pass.
